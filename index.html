Astrophysics
Exploring Exoplanets GJ-3470, WASP-107-b, and WASP-80-b with JWST and Eureka!
Using data from the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) Guaranteed Time Observations (GTO) program, I conducted an extensive data reduction and analysis of exoplanets using the Eureka! analysis pipeline. I processed and analyzed two transits of GJ-3470, one transit of WASP-107-b, one transit of WASP-80-b, and one eclipse of WASP-80-b. The primary objective was to confirm stellar and planetary properties and determine transit depths at the photometric wavelengths. This work was presented at NASA Ames and contributed to multiple publications.
Development of the Eureka! Pipeline
In collaboration with Taylor Bell and NASA Ames, I contributed to the development of the Eureka! analysis pipeline. Eureka! is a data reduction and analysis tool specifically designed for exoplanet time-series observations, with a particular emphasis on JWST data. Over the next 1-2 decades, JWST will focus on four main science themes: the Early Universe, Galaxies Over Time, the Star Lifecycle, and Other Worlds. Eureka! aims to provide the astronomy community with an open-source end-to-end pipeline tool for the reduction and analysis of exoplanet time-series observations, supporting the pursuit of the fourth theme, Other Worlds. This work was presented at NASA Ames and is used for JWST exoplanet analysis worldwide.

Exploring Aqu-MM4 through Infrared Keck Observations
Using data from the Keck Observatory, I conducted a comprehensive data reduction and analysis of the protostellar system Aqu-MM4 with three primary objectives. First, to model the infrared spectrum of the star using the Phoenix BT-Settl Model. Second, to match the model to the observations. Third, to determine parameter constraints. This work was presented at NASA Ames.
Exploring HD 189733 b (The Nighmare World) with JWST & Eclipse Mapping
Using data from the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) Guaranteed Time Observations (GTO) program, I conducted a thorough data reduction and analysis of exoplanet HD189733b's spectra with three primary objectives. First, to measure the 2D temperature and wind structure of the planet. Second, to assess the effectiveness of JWST. Third, to measure the 3D temperature, wind, and chemical structure of the planet's atmosphere. This work was presented at the Bay Area Exoplanet Meeting 2022 Oct. 28 (BEAM) and NASA Ames community.

A high internal heat flux and large core in a warm Neptune Exoplanet.

Abstract: Interactions between exoplanetary atmospheres and internal properties have long been hypothesized to be drivers of the inflation mechanisms of gaseous planets and apparent atmospheric chemical disequilibrium conditions1. However, transmission spectra of exoplanets has been limited in its ability to observational confirm these theories due to the limited wavelength coverage of HST and inferences of single molecules, mostly H2O (ref. 2). In this work, we present the panchromatic transmission spectrum of the approximately 750 K, low-density, Neptune-sized exoplanet WASP-107b using a combination of HST WFC3, JWST NIRCam and MIRI. From this spectrum, we detect spectroscopic features due to H2O (21σ), CH4 (5σ), CO (7σ), CO2 (29σ), SO2 (9σ), and NH3 (6σ). The presence of these molecules enable constraints on the atmospheric metal enrichment (M/H is 10–18× Solar3), vertical mixing strength (log10Kzz = 8.4–9.0 cm2s−1), and internal temperature (>345 K). The high internal temperature is suggestive of tidally-driven inflation4 acting upon a Neptunelike internal structure, which can naturally explain the planet’s large radius and low density. These findings suggest that eccentricity driven tidal heating is a critical process governing atmospheric chemistry and interior structure inferences for a majority of the cool (1,000K) super-Earth-to-Saturn mass exoplanet population.

Authors: Luis Welbanks, Taylor J. Bell, Thomas G. Beatty, Michael R. Line, Kazumasa Ohno, Jonathan J. Fortney, Everett Schlawin, Thomas P. The JWST emission spectrum of the inflated hot Saturn WASP-69 b.


Authors: Schlawin, Everett ; Mukherjee, Sagnick ; Ohno, Kazumasa ; Bell, Taylor ; Beatty, Thomas ; Line, Michael ; Fortney, Jonathan ; Welbanks, Luis ; Rauscher, Emily ; Wiser, Lindsey ; Parmentier, Vivien ; Murphy, Matthew ; Edelman, Isaac ; Challener, Ryan ; Greene, Thomas

Abstract: Emission spectra of exoplanet atmospheres give clues about their temperature structure, composition, vertical transport and geometric albedo, elucidating their chemical processes and giving clues about their formation and aerosols. We reveal the JWST 2-11 um emission spectrum of the hot Saturn WASP-69 b (960 K zero-albedo equilibrium temperature) over three eclipses as part of the MANATEE program. We find evidence for carbon dioxide, carbon monoxide and water vapor in the atmosphere. The features are seen in absorption and require a temperature profile that decreases with altitude. Chemical equilibrium models point to an atmosphere enriched in heavy elements relative to its host star. We also find an unexpectedly high planet-to-star flux ratio at the shortest wavelengths (below 3 um), indicating a possible high geometric albedo for the planet, but one that is hard to reconcile with the thermal emission of the planet. This spectrum opens a new window into hot Jupiter atmospheres in the sub-1000 K equilibrium temperature regime where chemical transitions are expected to be significant.

Searching for Molecular Jets from High-mass Protostars.

Abstract: We report Very Large Array observations in the Q band toward 10 ionized jet candidates to search for SiO emission, a well-known shocked gas tracer. We detected 7 mm continuum counterparts toward 90% of the jet candidates. In most cases, the jet candidate is located toward the center of the 7 mm core, and the high masses (≈100 M⊙) and densities (≈107 cm−3) of the cores suggest that the central objects are very young high-mass protostars. We detected SiO J = 1–0 emission associated with six target sources. In all cases, the morphology and spectrum of the emission is consistent with what is expected for molecular jets along an outflow axis, thus confirming the jet nature of 60% of our sample. Our data suggest a positive correlation between the SiO luminosity LSiO, and both the bolometric luminosity LBol and the radio luminosity Sνd2 of the driving sources.

Authors: Tatiana M. Rodríguez, Peter Hofner, Isaac Edelman, Esteban D. Araya, and Viviana Rosero

Searching for Molecular Jets from high-mass protostars.

Abstract: Even though high-mass stars (M>8 Msun) shape the universe at all scales, from their ionizing winds to their explosive deaths, how they are formed is still one of the biggest unknowns in astronomy. One proposed formation model is a scaled up version of the accretion model that seems to work well for lower mass objects. Jets are key ingredients in this formation scenario, since they dissipate excess angular momentum and allow accretion to proceed. However, observing massive young stellar objects (MYSOs) in their earliest protostellar stages is to this day a big observational challenge due to their rarity, large distances, and high extinction. To study the jet nature of the emission, we carried out VLA observations toward a sample of 10 ionized jet candidates at 42 GHz looking for SiO J=1-0 emission. In this talk we will present the preliminary results of this work.

Authors: Rodriguez, T. M. ; Hofner, P. ; Edelman, I. ; Araya, D. E. ; Rosero, V.

Applying modern meteorological tools to the study of Ball Lightning (Kugelblitz).

Abstract: Rakov and Uman pointed out (Lightning: Physics and Effects, 2003) that despite 5000 published reports of ball lightning (BL) and a scientific literature comparable in volume to the literature on conventional lightning, we still have no idea of what mechanisms create or power BL. Lightning characterization technology advances can and should be applied to BL studies. Keul and Diendorfer (2018) have correlated BL reports with European lightning-detection network data, but no such attempt has been made for US BL reports up to now. Using 31 BL reports from a now-defunct US website, we have attempted correlations with National Lightning Detection Network (NLDN) and radar data archived at the National Climactic Data Center. Of the 31 reports, 5 objects were indoors and 25 were reported to coincide with thunderstorms. Time information accurate to 30 minutes (1 sigma) and location information accurate within one to ten km was available from nine of the reports, and for these we obtained both NLDN and composite radar data covering the time frames indicated by the reports. For three of the nine reports, we found NLDN located strikes plausibly within the distance and time frames of the observer reports – thus qualifying as nearby (possibly causal) lightning. However, for the report for which the location of the ball was known within meters, the nearest lightning was 2.3 km away. 23 reports only associate BL with a storm within 25 km. Should it continue to hold in the face of more evidence, the relative lack of correlation of BL and nearby lightning suggests that the production of BL is associated more with static or changing electric fields in the vicinity of thunderstorms, than with the lightning plasma channel itself. While these results by themselves are limited, we are optimistic that one can learn more about the link between natural and ball lightning by fusing more precise eyewitness reports with lightning location and other archival meteorological data. To this end, we have launched a website to guide citizen scientists in future ball lightning reports. The site may be reached via https://tinyurl.com/BLReport.

Authors: Richard Sonnenfeld, Karl Stephan, Alexander Keul, Isaac Edelman, Sergio Jimenez
